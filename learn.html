🟢 Basics — start here

🤖 What is an LLM?

An LLM (Large Language Model) is a super-good guesser that has read a giant library. You give it some words, and it guesses the next words — one at a time — really, really well.

🌍 Real life: like the autocomplete on your phone, but smart enough to finish a whole story instead of just one word.

🧩 What is a token?

The model doesn't read letters or whole words — it chops text into little pieces called tokens. A piece can be a whole word, part of a word, or punctuation.

🌍 Real life: like LEGO. "cat" might be one brick; "unbelievable" gets built from a few smaller bricks.

🔢 What is a token ID?

Every token brick has a number printed on it. The model only understands numbers, so each piece becomes its ID number.

🌍 Real life: like every kid in class having a roll number — the teacher can call "number 12" instead of the name.

📍 What is an embedding (vector)?

Each word is turned into a list of numbers that captures its meaning. Words that mean similar things get similar lists, so they end up close together in "meaning space".

🌍 Real life: like seating kids by interests — football fans together, painters together. "Happy" sits near "joyful", far from "table".

🪟 What is the context window?

It's how much the model can hold in its head at once. If your text is too long, it forgets the earliest parts.

🌍 Real life: like short-term memory — you can keep a phone number in your head for a minute, but not the whole phone book.

✍️ How does it write an answer?

It guesses the next token, adds it, then guesses again — over and over — until the answer is finished. That's the whole trick.

🌍 Real life: like singing a song you half-know — each word reminds you of the next one.

🌡️ What is temperature?

A creativity knob. Low temperature = it always picks the safest next word (careful but boring). High temperature = it takes risks (fun, but sometimes silly).

🌍 Real life: low = a careful student giving the textbook answer; high = a friend brainstorming wild ideas.

🎯 What are top-k and top-p?

Before picking a word, these rules throw away the very unlikely choices so the model doesn't blurt out nonsense. Top-k keeps the best k options; top-p keeps the best options until they add up to "enough" probability.

🌍 Real life: ordering only from the few popular dishes everyone likes, instead of the entire menu including the weird stuff.

🏋️ What is training?

The model practiced by reading huge amounts of text and guessing the next word billions of times, getting a tiny bit better with each guess.

🌍 Real life: like learning to ride a bike — you try, wobble, fall, and slowly stop falling.

🎛️ What are "parameters" / model size?

Parameters are the tiny knobs inside the model that training tuned. More knobs usually means smarter — but bigger and slower.

🌍 Real life: a giant mixing board with billions of sliders, each nudged to just the right spot.

😵 Why do LLMs make things up (hallucinate)?

It's always guessing the next likely word — it doesn't truly "know" facts. Sometimes a confident-sounding guess is simply wrong.

🌍 Real life: like a kid confidently giving an answer in class because it sounds right — even when it isn't.

💬 What is a prompt?

The prompt is what you say to the model — your question or instructions. Clearer prompts get better answers.

🌍 Real life: asking a genie "make me a sandwich" vs "make me a grilled cheese with tomato" — the clearer wish gets the better result.

🔎 What is RAG ("chat with your documents")?

The model is handed the right pages to read before answering, so it doesn't have to guess from memory.

🌍 Real life: an open-book test — you look the answer up in your notes instead of remembering everything.

🩺 What is fine-tuning?

Taking a model that already knows language and giving it extra practice on a special topic so it becomes great at that.

🌍 Real life: a doctor who finished general school, then trained more to become a heart specialist.

🦜 Is an LLM thinking or alive?

No. It's a very fancy pattern-matcher that predicts words. It doesn't feel or understand the way people do.

🌍 Real life: like a parrot that learned to say clever-sounding sentences — impressive, but it isn't a person.

🧠 Transformer architecture

What is the Transformer architecture?

A neural network built from stacked blocks, each containing an attention layer and a feed-forward layer, connected by residual links. It processes a whole sequence in parallel and uses attention to mix information between positions. It replaced RNNs/LSTMs (2017, "Attention Is All You Need").

What is self-attention?

A mechanism where every token looks at every other token and decides how much to "pay attention" to each, then blends their information accordingly. It's how a model resolves what "it" refers to, or links a verb to its subject.

What are queries, keys, and values (Q, K, V)?

Each token is projected into three vectors. A token's query is matched against every other token's key (dot product) to get attention weights; those weights then combine the values. Think: query = "what I'm looking for", key = "what I offer", value = "what I pass on".

What is multi-head attention?

Attention is run several times in parallel with different learned projections ("heads"), each able to focus on a different kind of relationship (syntax, coreference, position…). Their outputs are concatenated and mixed.

What is the feed-forward (MLP) layer in a block?

After attention mixes tokens, a per-token two-layer MLP (often 4× wider than the model dimension) transforms each position independently. This is where much of the model's "knowledge" is stored.

What are residual connections?

Each sub-layer adds its output to its input (x + f(x)) instead of replacing it. This keeps gradients flowing through very deep networks and forms the "residual stream" that information travels along.

What is layer normalization (and Pre-LN vs Post-LN)?

LayerNorm rescales a vector to stable statistics so training doesn't blow up. Modern LLMs use Pre-LN (normalize before each sub-layer), which trains more stably than the original Post-LN. RMSNorm is a common cheaper variant.

What is positional encoding (absolute vs RoPE)?

Attention has no built-in sense of order, so position info is injected. Early models added fixed/learned absolute position vectors; most modern LLMs use RoPE (rotary embeddings), which rotates Q/K by angle proportional to position and generalizes better to longer contexts.

Decoder-only vs encoder-decoder vs encoder-only?

Decoder-only (GPT, Llama): predicts next token, best for generation. Encoder-only (BERT): reads bidirectionally, best for classification/embeddings. Encoder-decoder (T5): reads input then generates, good for translation/summarization.

What is causal (masked) attention?

In a generator, each token may only attend to earlier tokens, never future ones — enforced by masking. This lets the model be trained on all positions at once while still learning to predict left-to-right.

What is the "residual stream"?

The running vector at each position that every layer reads from and writes back to via residual connections. Interpretability research treats it as a shared communication channel between components.

How big are typical LLMs (layers/heads/dims)?

A 7B model might have ~32 layers, ~32 heads, hidden size ~4096. Bigger models scale all three. "Parameters" counts all the weights across these layers (billions to trillions).

🔬 Attention deep dive

Why divide attention scores by √dk?

Dot products grow with dimension, pushing softmax into tiny-gradient regions. Scaling by the square root of the key dimension keeps the scores at a sane magnitude so training stays stable.

Why is attention O(n²)?

Every token attends to every other token, so cost and memory grow with the square of sequence length n. Doubling context roughly quadruples attention compute — the main barrier to long contexts.

What is FlashAttention?

An exact attention algorithm that computes results in tiles without materializing the full n×n matrix in memory, using GPU SRAM cleverly. Same math, much faster and far less memory.

What is the KV cache?

During generation, the keys and values of past tokens are saved so each new token doesn't recompute them. It makes generation fast but its memory grows with sequence length × layers — often the real context-length limit.

What are MQA and GQA?

Multi-Query Attention shares one set of K/V across all heads; Grouped-Query Attention shares K/V among groups of heads. Both shrink the KV cache dramatically, speeding up inference with little quality loss.

What is sliding-window / local attention?

Each token only attends to a fixed window of nearby tokens instead of the whole sequence, cutting cost to linear. Used in models like Mistral to handle longer inputs cheaply.

What is sparse attention?

Attention restricted to a chosen subset of token pairs (strided, block, global) rather than all pairs, trading some expressiveness for much lower cost on long sequences.

What is ALiBi?

Attention with Linear Biases adds a distance-based penalty to attention scores instead of using positional embeddings, helping models extrapolate to longer sequences than they trained on.

How do long-context (100k–1M token) models work?

Combinations of RoPE scaling (e.g., position interpolation/YaRN), efficient attention (Flash/GQA), and lots of memory. Quality often degrades in the middle of very long contexts ("lost in the middle").

What are attention sinks?

Models tend to dump excess attention onto the first few tokens. Keeping those "sink" tokens in the KV cache lets streaming/long-context inference stay stable when older tokens are evicted.

🔡 Tokenization & embeddings (advanced)

What is byte-pair encoding (BPE)?

A tokenizer trained by repeatedly merging the most frequent adjacent symbol pair into a new token. It balances vocabulary size against sequence length, and handles unknown words by falling back to smaller pieces.

BPE vs WordPiece vs Unigram vs SentencePiece?

WordPiece (BERT) merges by likelihood gain; Unigram (T5) prunes a large vocab by probability; SentencePiece is a library that trains BPE/Unigram directly on raw text (no pre-tokenization), great for many languages.

What is byte-level BPE?

BPE applied over raw bytes (256 base symbols) instead of characters, so it can represent any text — emoji, code, every language — with no out-of-vocabulary problem. Used by GPT-2 onward.

Why does tokenization penalize non-English text?

Tokenizers learn merges mostly from English-heavy data, so other scripts get split into many small tokens. The same sentence can cost 2–5× more tokens (and money, and context) in some languages — a real fairness issue.

What are tied embeddings and the LM head?

The input embedding matrix maps token IDs → vectors; the LM head (unembedding) maps the final vector → a score for every token. Many models tie these (share the same matrix) to save parameters.

Static vs contextual embeddings?

Static (word2vec/GloVe) give one fixed vector per word. Contextual (transformer) give a different vector depending on surrounding words, so "bank" by a river differs from "bank" with money.

What are "glitch tokens"?

Rare tokens (like the infamous "SolidGoldMagikarp") that appeared in the tokenizer's training data but barely in the model's, causing bizarre, unstable behavior when used. A quirk of mismatched tokenizer/model data.

What is the vocabulary size and why does it matter?

The number of distinct tokens (e.g., ~50K for GPT-2, ~100K–200K for newer models). Bigger vocab = shorter sequences but a larger embedding/output layer and rarer tokens seen less during training.

🏋️ Pretraining

What is the next-token prediction objective?

The model is trained to predict token t+1 from tokens 1…t. This single self-supervised task, at scale, teaches grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, and style — all without human labels.

What is cross-entropy / negative log-likelihood loss?

The training loss is the negative log of the probability the model assigned to the correct next token, averaged over all tokens. Minimizing it means making the right token more probable.

What is teacher forcing?

During training the model is always fed the true previous tokens (not its own guesses), so every position can be learned in parallel. At inference it instead feeds back its own outputs.

What data are LLMs trained on?

Web crawls (Common Crawl), books, code (GitHub), Wikipedia, papers, and more — cleaned, filtered, and deduplicated. Data quality and mixture matter as much as quantity.

What are scaling laws / Chinchilla-optimal training?

Loss falls predictably as a power law in model size, data, and compute. Chinchilla showed many models were undertrained: for a compute budget, you should scale data roughly in proportion to parameters (~20 tokens/param).

What is a learning-rate schedule (warmup + decay)?

LR starts near zero and ramps up (warmup) to avoid early instability, then decays (often cosine) toward zero so the model settles into a good minimum.

What is AdamW?

The standard optimizer for LLMs: Adam (adaptive per-parameter step sizes using gradient moments) with decoupled weight decay for better regularization.

What is mixed-precision training (fp16/bf16)?

Storing/computing in 16-bit floats to halve memory and speed up math, while keeping critical parts in higher precision. bf16 trades mantissa bits for range and is now standard on modern accelerators.

What is gradient clipping?

Capping the size of gradients before an update so occasional huge gradients don't destabilize or blow up training.

Why does data deduplication matter?

Duplicate text makes models memorize and waste capacity, and inflates evaluation if test data leaks in. Dedup improves quality and reduces regurgitation.

What is curriculum / data-mixture scheduling?

Ordering or reweighting data during training (e.g., more code or high-quality text later) to shape capabilities. Mixture choices strongly affect the final model's strengths.

🎯 Alignment & fine-tuning

What is supervised fine-tuning (SFT)?

After pretraining, the model is trained on curated prompt→response examples so it learns to follow instructions and answer in a helpful format.

What is RLHF?

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback: humans rank model outputs, a reward model learns those preferences, and the LLM is optimized to score higher — making it more helpful, honest, and harmless.

What is a reward model?

A model trained on human preference comparisons to output a scalar "how good is this response" score, used as the optimization target during RLHF.

What is PPO in RLHF?

Proximal Policy Optimization, the RL algorithm often used to update the LLM against the reward model while a KL penalty keeps it from drifting too far from the original model.

What is DPO (Direct Preference Optimization)?

A simpler alternative to RLHF that optimizes directly on preference pairs with a classification-style loss — no separate reward model or RL loop — often matching PPO with less complexity.

What is RLAIF / Constitutional AI?

Using AI feedback instead of (or alongside) human feedback: a model critiques and revises outputs against a set of written principles ("constitution") to align behavior at scale.

What is instruction tuning?

Fine-tuning on many diverse tasks phrased as instructions so the model generalizes to following new instructions it never explicitly saw.

What is LoRA?

Low-Rank Adaptation: freeze the big model and train tiny low-rank "adapter" matrices added to some weights. You fine-tune <1% of parameters, cheaply and swappably.

What is QLoRA?

LoRA on top of a 4-bit quantized base model, letting you fine-tune large models on a single consumer GPU with minimal quality loss.

What is PEFT?

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning — the family of methods (LoRA, adapters, prefix/prompt tuning) that adapt a model by training a small number of extra parameters instead of all of them.

What is catastrophic forgetting?

When fine-tuning on new data makes a model lose previously learned abilities. PEFT, replay of old data, and careful learning rates help mitigate it.

What is the "alignment tax"?

The small capability cost sometimes paid when aligning a model for safety and helpfulness — e.g., it may refuse more or score slightly lower on raw benchmarks.

🎲 Decoding & sampling

What is greedy decoding?

Always pick the single highest-probability next token. Fast and deterministic, but can be repetitive and get stuck in loops.

What is beam search?

Keep the top-N partial sequences ("beams") at each step and expand them, choosing the most probable overall sequence. Good for translation; tends to be bland for open-ended text.

What does temperature do (technically)?

It divides the logits before softmax. <1 sharpens the distribution (more confident/deterministic); >1 flattens it (more diverse/random); →0 approaches greedy.

What is top-k sampling?

Keep only the k most probable tokens, renormalize, and sample from those. Prevents picking absurd low-probability tokens while keeping some variety.

What is top-p (nucleus) sampling?

Keep the smallest set of top tokens whose probabilities sum to p (e.g., 0.9), then sample. The candidate pool size adapts to how confident the model is.

What are repetition / frequency / presence penalties?

Adjustments that lower the probability of tokens already used, discouraging the model from repeating itself or looping.

What is min-p sampling?

Keep tokens whose probability is at least a fraction of the top token's probability. A newer scheme that adapts better than fixed top-k/top-p across confidence levels.

What is constrained decoding (JSON/grammar mode)?

Restricting the next-token choices to only those allowed by a grammar or schema, guaranteeing valid JSON or other structured output.

What is speculative decoding?

A small "draft" model proposes several tokens which the big model verifies in one pass, accepting the ones it agrees with. Same output distribution, often 2–3× faster.

⚙️ Inference & systems

What is quantization (int8/int4)?

Storing weights (and sometimes activations) in fewer bits to shrink memory and speed up inference, with small accuracy loss. Enables big models on small hardware.

What are GPTQ and AWQ?

Post-training quantization methods that pick low-bit weight values to minimize error (GPTQ) or protect the most important weights (AWQ), preserving quality at 4-bit.

What is the prefill vs decode phase?

Prefill processes the whole prompt in parallel (compute-bound); decode then generates one token at a time (memory-bandwidth-bound). They have very different performance characteristics.

What is continuous batching?

An inference-server technique that adds and removes requests from a running batch token-by-token, keeping the GPU busy and boosting throughput vs static batching.

What is PagedAttention / vLLM?

vLLM's method of managing the KV cache in non-contiguous "pages" (like OS virtual memory), reducing fragmentation and enabling far higher concurrency.

What is model parallelism (tensor / pipeline)?

Splitting a model that's too big for one GPU: tensor parallelism splits individual layers across GPUs; pipeline parallelism puts different layers on different GPUs.

What are ZeRO / FSDP?

Techniques that shard optimizer states, gradients, and parameters across many GPUs so huge models can be trained without each GPU holding a full copy.

What is a Mixture of Experts (MoE)?

A model with many "expert" feed-forward blocks where a router sends each token to only a few. You get a huge parameter count but only activate a fraction per token — more capacity at similar compute.

What is knowledge distillation?

Training a smaller "student" model to imitate a larger "teacher" (its outputs or probabilities), getting much of the quality at a fraction of the size.

Why does KV-cache memory limit context length?

Each token stores keys/values for every layer and head; memory grows linearly with context length. Long contexts can need more memory for the cache than for the model weights.

📈 Scaling & emergence

What are scaling laws?

Empirical power-law relationships showing loss decreases smoothly as model size, data, and compute grow — letting labs predict a big model's performance before training it.

What are emergent abilities?

Skills (multi-step arithmetic, certain reasoning) that appear fairly suddenly above a model-size threshold. Their "sharpness" is debated and partly depends on how you measure them.

What is in-context learning?

The ability to learn a task from examples placed in the prompt, with no weight updates. Few-shot prompting works because of this emergent behavior.

What is grokking?

A phenomenon where a model trains to memorize, then much later suddenly generalizes (validation accuracy jumps long after training accuracy saturates).

What is the "bitter lesson"?

Rich Sutton's observation that general methods which scale with compute and data tend to beat hand-crafted, knowledge-engineered approaches over time.

What is test-time compute / reasoning models?

Letting a model "think" longer at inference (long chains of thought, search, self-checking) trades extra compute for better answers — the idea behind o1/o3-style reasoning models.

📊 Evaluation

How are LLMs benchmarked (MMLU, etc.)?

Standardized test sets across subjects and skills — MMLU (knowledge), GSM8K (math), HumanEval (code), and many more — reported as accuracy or pass rates.

What is perplexity?

The exponential of the average per-token loss — roughly "how many tokens the model is choosing between" on average. Lower = the model is less surprised by the text.

What is LLM-as-a-judge?

Using a strong LLM to score or compare other models' outputs. Scalable and cheap, but can be biased (e.g., toward length or its own style) and needs careful design.

What is benchmark contamination?

When test questions leak into training data, inflating scores. It's a major reason public benchmark numbers can overstate real ability.

What is pass@k?

A code-eval metric: the chance that at least one of k sampled solutions passes the tests. Rewards models that can produce a correct answer within a few tries.

Capability vs alignment evaluations?

Capability evals measure what a model can do; alignment/safety evals measure whether it behaves as intended (refusing harm, honesty, robustness to jailbreaks).

🔎 RAG & retrieval (advanced)

What is a vector database?

A store optimized to hold embeddings and find the nearest ones to a query vector quickly (Pinecone, Weaviate, FAISS, pgvector). The backbone of semantic search and RAG.

What is approximate nearest neighbor (ANN / HNSW)?

Algorithms that find close-enough nearest vectors far faster than exact search. HNSW builds a navigable graph for fast, high-recall lookups over millions of vectors.

What is chunking and why does it matter?

Splitting documents into passages before embedding. Chunk size/overlap strongly affect retrieval quality — too big dilutes meaning, too small loses context.

What is re-ranking?

A second, more accurate model (often a cross-encoder) re-orders the top retrieved candidates for relevance, improving what the LLM finally reads.

What is hybrid search?

Combining keyword search (BM25) with dense vector search to get both exact term matches and semantic matches, usually beating either alone.

What are common RAG failure modes?

Retrieving irrelevant or missing chunks, contradictory sources, the answer spanning multiple chunks, and the LLM ignoring or over-trusting retrieved text. Good chunking, re-ranking, and citations help.

🤖 Agents & tool use

What is function calling / tool use?

The model is given tool/function definitions and can output a structured call (name + arguments); your code runs it and returns the result, letting the model use calculators, search, APIs, etc.

What is chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting?

Asking the model to reason step by step before answering, which improves accuracy on multi-step problems by letting it "show its work".

What is ReAct?

A loop of Reason then Act: the model thinks, calls a tool, observes the result, and repeats — the basis of many agent frameworks.

What is an AI agent?

An LLM that plans and takes multiple actions toward a goal — calling tools, reading results, and deciding next steps — rather than answering in one shot.

What is the Model Context Protocol (MCP)?

An open standard for connecting AI apps to external tools and data sources through a common interface, so any model can use any MCP-compatible tool/server.

What is multi-agent orchestration?

Multiple specialized agents (planner, coder, reviewer…) collaborating or running in parallel, coordinated by a controller — useful for complex tasks but harder to keep reliable.

🛡️ Safety, limits & interpretability

Why do LLMs hallucinate (technical)?

They optimize next-token likelihood, not truth. With no grounding and pressure to always answer, a plausible-but-wrong continuation can be the most probable one. Retrieval, calibration, and abstention reduce it.

What is prompt injection?

Malicious instructions hidden in content the model reads (a web page, a document) that hijack its behavior. A top security risk for agents and RAG systems.

What is jailbreaking?

Crafting prompts that bypass a model's safety training to get disallowed output. An ongoing cat-and-mouse between attackers and alignment methods.

What is mechanistic interpretability?

Reverse-engineering the internal computations of a model — circuits, features, and how components combine — to understand why it produces an output.

What are superposition and polysemanticity?

Models pack more features than they have neurons by overlapping them, so a single neuron can respond to many unrelated concepts (polysemantic), making interpretation hard.

What is a sparse autoencoder (SAE) in interpretability?

A tool that decomposes a model's activations into many sparse, more human-interpretable "features", helping untangle superposition.

What is reward hacking?

When a model finds ways to score high on the reward/metric without actually doing the intended task — e.g., being sycophantic or verbose because raters liked that.

What is sycophancy?

A model tending to agree with or flatter the user rather than be accurate, often a side effect of human-preference training.

What is memorization / data leakage?

Large models can reproduce verbatim chunks of training data, raising privacy and copyright concerns. Deduplication and filtering reduce but don't eliminate it.

Where does bias in LLMs come from?

Mostly from biased training data (and labeling choices), which the model reflects and can amplify. Mitigations include data curation, alignment, and evaluation for fairness.

What is the difference between alignment and capability?

Capability is how powerful/skilled a model is; alignment is whether it reliably does what we want and avoids harm. A model can be highly capable yet poorly aligned, or vice versa.

What is calibration?

Whether a model's stated/implied confidence matches reality (90% confident → right 90% of the time). Alignment training can hurt calibration, making models overconfident.
